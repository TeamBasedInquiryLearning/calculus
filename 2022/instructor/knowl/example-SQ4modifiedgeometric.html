Example 8.4.5.

Given a series that appears to be mostly geometric:

\begin{equation*} 3+(1.1)^3+(1.1)^4+\cdots(1.1)^n+\cdots \end{equation*}

we can rewrite it as the sum of a standard geometric series with some modification: \(\displaystyle ???+\sum_{n=0}^\infty a\cdot r^n\text{:}\)

\begin{align*} &3+(1.1)^3+(1.1)^4+\cdots(1.1)^n+\cdots \\ &=3-\textcolor{blue}{((1.1)^0+(1.1)^1+(1.1)^2)+((1.1)^0+(1.1)^1+(1.1)^2)}+(1.1)^3+\cdots\\ &=3-((1.1)^0+(1.1)^1+(1.1)^2)+(1.1)^0+(1.1)^1+(1.1)^2+(1.1)^3+\cdots\\ &=3-((1.1)^0+(1.1)^1+(1.1)^2)+\sum_{n=0}^\infty (1.1)^n\\ &=-0.31+\sum_{n=0}^\infty (1.1)^n \end{align*}

which converges if and only if \(\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^\infty (1.1)^n\) converges. In this case, it does not.
