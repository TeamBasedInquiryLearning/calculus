Section 5.4 Trigonometric Substitution (TI4)

Learning Outcomes

  • Use trigonometric substitution to compute indefinite integrals.

Activity 5.4.1.

Consider the integral \(\displaystyle \int \frac{\sqrt{1-x^2}}{x} dx\text{.}\) If one were to use the method of substitution to evaluate this integral, what would be the best choice of \(u\text{?}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle u=1-x^2.\)

  2. \(\displaystyle u=\sqrt{1-x^2}.\)

  3. \(\displaystyle u=\frac{1}{x}.\)

  4. There is no u for which the method of substitution will work.

Activity 5.4.2.

It would be good if \(\sqrt{1-x^2}\) were a term that can be written more simply. Which of the following mathematical facts could help us simplify this integrand?

  1. For any real number, \(x^2\) must be positive (note: this isn’t even true).

  2. \(\displaystyle u=\sqrt{1-x^2}.\)

  3. The Pythagorean theorem allows us to write \((\cos(\theta))^2\) as \(1-(\sin(\theta))^2\text{.}\)

  4. The ratio of an even function and an odd function is odd.

Activity 5.4.3.

Suppose we let \(x=\sin(\theta)\text{,}\) then \(\frac{dx}{d\theta}=\cos(\theta)\) and \(dx=\cos(\theta) d\theta\text{.}\) Which of the following best represents the integral \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{1-x^2}dx\text{?}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{1-\sin^2(\theta)}dx\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{ 1-\sin^2(\theta)\cos(\theta)}d\theta\text{.}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{ 1-\sin^2(\theta)}\cos(\theta)d\theta\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.4.

Use the fact from Activity 5.4.2 to rewrite your choice of integral from Activity 5.4.3 to an integral without any \(\sin(\theta)\) terms.

  1. \(\displaystyle\int \cos(\theta) d\theta\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle\int \cos^2(\theta) d\theta\text{.}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle\int \sqrt{\cos(\theta)} d\theta\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.5.

Use the fact that \(\cos^2(\theta) =\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2}\) to rewrite the integral from Activity 5.4.4.

  1. \(\displaystyle\int \sqrt{\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2}} d\theta\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle\int \frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2} d\theta\text{.}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle\int \left(\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2} \right)^2d\theta\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.6.

Evaluate the integral in terms of \(\theta\) that you found in Activity 5.4.5.

Activity 5.4.7.

Consider the fact that \(x=\sin(\theta)\) and thus \(\theta=\arcsin(x)\text{.}\) Use this to rewrite the solution you found in Activity 5.4.6 in terms of \(x\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.8.

Consider the integral \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{9-x^2} dx\text{;}\) how can we best modify our approach in Activity 5.4.3 and adapt it here?

  1. Let \(x=\sin(\theta), dx=\cos(\theta)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \sqrt{9-x^2} dx= \int \sqrt{9-\sin^2(\theta)} \cos(\theta) d\theta.\)

  2. Let \(x=3\sin(\theta), dx=3\cos(\theta)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \sqrt{9-x^2} dx= \int \sqrt{9-(3\sin(\theta))^2} \cos(\theta) d\theta.\)

  3. Let \(x=\sin(3\theta), dx=3\cos(3\theta)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \sqrt{9-x^2} dx= \int \sqrt{9-\sin^2(3\theta)} 3\cos(3\theta) d\theta.\)

Activity 5.4.9.

Using your choice of substitution in Activity 5.4.8 and the fact that \(\cos^2(\theta) =\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2}\) to rewrite the integral from Activity 5.4.8

  1. \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \int \sqrt{\frac{9+9\cos(2\theta)}{2}} \cos(\theta) d\theta.\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \int \frac{9+9\cos(2\theta)}{2} \cos(\theta) d\theta.\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \displaystyle \int \left(\frac{9+9\cos(2\theta)}{2}\right)^2 \cos(\theta) d\theta.\)

Activity 5.4.10.

Evaluate the integral that you found in Activity 5.4.9. (Recall that since \(x=3\sin(\theta), \theta=\arcsin\left(\frac{x}{3}\right)\text{.}\))

Activity 5.4.11.

Consider the integral \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{1+x^2} dx\text{.}\) Which of the following facts would best simplify this expression?

  1. \(\cos^2(\theta) =\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2}\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle (\cos(\theta))^2=1-(\sin(\theta))^2\)

  3. Since \(\sin^2(\theta) + \cos^2(\theta)=1, \tan^2(\theta)+1=\sec^2(\theta)\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.12.

Suppose we let \(x=\tan(\theta), dx =\sec^2(\theta)\text{.}\) Which of the following best represents the integral \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{1+x^2} dx\text{?}\)

  1. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{1+\tan^2(\theta)} dx\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{1+\tan^2(\theta)\sec^2(\theta)} d\theta\text{.}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{1+\tan^2(\theta)} \sec^2(\theta)d\theta\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.13.

Using the fact from Activity 5.4.11, simplify and evaluate your choice of integral from Activity 5.4.12. (Recall that since \(x=\tan(\theta), \theta=\arctan(x)\text{.}\))

Activity 5.4.14.

Consider the integral \(\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+x^2} dx\text{;}\) how can we best modify our approach in Activity 5.4.12 and adapt it here?

  1. Let \(x=\frac{1}{2}\tan(\theta), dx=\frac{1}{2}\sec^2(\theta)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+x^2} dx = \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+\left(\frac{1}{2}\tan(\theta) \right)^2} \frac{1}{2} \sec^2(\theta)d\theta.\)

  2. Let \(x=\frac{1}{4}\tan(\theta), dx=\frac{1}{4}\sec^2(\theta)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+x^2} dx = \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+\left(\frac{1}{4}\tan(\theta) \right)^2} \frac{1}{4}\sec^2(\theta) d\theta.\)

  3. Let \(x=\tan\left(\frac{1}{2}\theta\right), dx=\sec^2\left(\frac{1}{2}\theta\right)d\theta, \displaystyle \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+x^2} dx = \int \frac{1}{\frac{1}{4}+\left(\tan\left(\frac{1}{2}\theta\right) \right)^2} \frac{1}{2} \sec^2\left(\frac{1}{2}\theta\right)d\theta.\)

Activity 5.4.15.

Evaluate the choice of integral you found in Activity 5.4.8, using the fact that if \(x=\frac{1}{2}\tan(\theta), \theta=\arctan(2x)\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.16.

Consider the integral \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{x^2-1} dx\text{.}\) Which of the following facts best simplifies this integral?

  1. \(\cos^2(\theta) =\frac{1+\cos(2\theta)}{2}\text{.}\)

  2. \(\tan^2(\theta)+1=\sec^2(\theta)\text{.}\)

  3. Since \(\sin^2(\theta) =1- \cos^2(\theta), \tan^2(\theta)=\sec^2(\theta)-1\text{.}\)

Activity 5.4.17.

Using your choice in Activity 5.4.16, pick an appropriate trignometric substitution for \(x\) and rewrite \(\displaystyle \int \sqrt{x^2-1} dx\) as an integral in terms of \(\theta\text{.}\) (Remember to compute \(dx\) as well!)

Activity 5.4.18.

Evaluate the integral you found in Activity 5.4.17, using the fact that if \(x=\sec(\theta), \theta=\arcsec(x)\text{.}\)

Hint.

To better understand the relationship between \(x\) and functions of \(\theta\text{,}\) consider this right triangle where \(\sec(\theta)=x\text{.}\)

  2. \(\displaystyle x=k\tan(\theta)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle x=k\sec(\theta)\)

  2. \(\displaystyle x=k\tan(\theta)\)

Activity 5.4.21.

Activity 5.4.22.

Consider the unit circle \(x^2+y^2=1\text{.}\) Find a function \(f(x)\) so that \(y=f(x)\) is the graph of the upper-half semicircle of the unit circle.
