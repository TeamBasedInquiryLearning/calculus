Activity 4.8.4.

We now look for a general way of writing definite integrals for the area between two given curves, \(f(x)\) and \(g(x)\text{.}\) Consider this area, illustrated in Figure 103.
(a)
How could we represent the shaded area in Figure 103?
  1. \(\displaystyle \int_b^a f(x) \,dx - \int_b^a g(x) \,dx\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x) \,dx - \int_a^b g(x) \,dx\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \int_b^a g(x) \,dx - \int_b^a f(x) \,dx\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \int_a^b g(x) \,dx - \int_a^b f(x) \,dx\)
(b)
The two definite integrals above can be rewritten as one definite integral using the sum and difference property of definite integrals:
If \(f\) and \(g\) are continuous functions, then
\begin{equation*} \int_a^b ( f(x) \pm g(x)) \,dx = \int_a^b f(x) \,dx \pm \int_a^b g(x) \,dx \end{equation*}
Use the property above to represent the shaded area in Figure 103 using one definite integral.
  1. \(\displaystyle \int_b^a (f(x) - g(x)) \,dx\)
  2. \(\displaystyle \int_a^b (f(x) - g(x)) \,dx\)
  3. \(\displaystyle \int_b^a (g(x) - f(x)) \,dx\)
  4. \(\displaystyle \int_a^b (g(x) - f(x)) \,dx\)
