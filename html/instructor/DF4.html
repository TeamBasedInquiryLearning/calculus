Section 2.4 The product and quotient rules (DF4)

Learning Outcomes

  • Compute derivatives using the Product and Quotient Rules.

Activity 2.4.1.

Let \(f\) and \(g\) be the functions defined by \(f(t) = 2t^2\) and \(g(t) = t^3 + 4t\text{.}\)

(a)

Determine \(f'(t)\) and \(g'(t)\text{.}\)

(b)

Let \(p(t) = 2t^2 (t^3 + 4t)\) and observe that \(p(t) = f(t) \cdot g(t)\text{.}\) Rewrite the formula for \(p\) by distributing the \(2t^2\) term. Then, compute \(p'(t)\) using the sum and constant multiple rules.

(c)

True or false: \(p'(t) = f'(t) \cdot g'(t)\text{.}\)

Theorem 2.4.2. Product Rule.

If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, then their product \(P(x) = f(x) \cdot g(x)\) is also a differentiable function, and

\begin{equation*} P'(x) = f(x) g'(x) + g(x) f'(x)\text{.} \end{equation*}

Activity 2.4.3.

The product rule is a powerful tool, but sometimes it isn’t necessary; a more elementary rule may suffice. Which of the following products could you find the derivative of without using the product rule? Select all that apply.

  1. \(\displaystyle f(x)=e^x \sin x\)

  2. \(\displaystyle f(x)=\sqrt{x}(x^3+3x-3)\)

  3. \(\displaystyle f(x)=(4)(x^5)\)

  4. \(\displaystyle f(x)=x \ln x\)

Activity 2.4.5.

Let \(f\) and \(g\) be the functions defined by \(f(t) = 2t^2\) and \(g(t) = t^3 + 4t\text{.}\)

(a)

Determine \(f'(t)\) and \(g'(t)\text{.}\) (These were found previously in Activity 2.4.1.)

(b)

Let \(q(t) = \frac{t^3 + 4t}{2t^2}\) and observe that \(q(t) = \frac{g(t)}{f(t)}\text{.}\) Rewrite the formula for \(q\) by dividing each term in the numerator by the denominator and simplify to write \(q\) as a sum of constant multiples of powers of \(t\text{.}\) Then, compute \(q'(t)\) using the sum and constant multiple rules.

(c)

True or false: \(q'(t) = \frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}\text{.}\)

Theorem 2.4.6. Quotient Rule.

If \(f\) and \(g\) are differentiable functions, then their quotient \(Q(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) is also a differentiable function for all \(x\) where \(g(x) \ne 0\) and

\begin{equation*} Q'(x) = \frac{g(x) f'(x) - f(x) g'(x)}{g(x)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

Activity 2.4.9.

Demonstrate and explain how to find the derivative of the following functions. Be sure to explicitly denote which derivative rules (product, quotient, sum and difference, etc.) you are using in your work.

  1. \begin{equation*} f(w)= -\frac{3 \, w^{2} + 5 \, w - 2}{\sin\left(w\right)} \end{equation*}

  2. \begin{equation*} g(t)= \frac{t^{2} + 6 \, t + 1}{t^{2}} \end{equation*}

  3. \begin{equation*} h(t)= -2 \, {\left(t^{2} + 3 \, t + 3\right)} \cos\left(t\right) \end{equation*}

Answer.

  1. \begin{equation*} f' (w)= \frac{{\left(3 \, w^{2} + 5 \, w - 2\right)} \cos\left(w\right)}{\sin\left(w\right)^{2}} - \frac{6 \, w + 5}{\sin\left(w\right)} \end{equation*}

  2. \begin{equation*} g' (t)= -\frac{2 \, {\left(3 \, t + 1\right)}}{t^{3}} \end{equation*}

  3. \begin{equation*} h' (t)= -2 \, {\left(2 \, t + 3\right)} \cos\left(t\right) + 2 \, {\left(t^{2} + 3 \, t + 3\right)} \sin\left(t\right) \end{equation*}

Note 2.4.10.

We have found the derivatives of \(\sin x\) and \(\cos x\text{,}\) but what about the other trigonometric functions? It turns out that the quotient rule along with some trig identities can help us! (See https://www.khanacademy.org/math/trigonometry/trig-equations-and-identities/using-trig-identities/a/trig-identity-reference for a reminder of the reciprocal and Pythagorean identities.)

Activity 2.4.11.

\begin{equation*} f'(x) = \frac{(\cos x)(\cos x) + (\sin x)(\sin x)}{(\cos x)^2}\text{.} \end{equation*}

(e)

Remark 2.4.15.

We can now summarize the derivatives for all six trigonometric functions.

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \sin x = \cos x\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \cos x = -\sin x\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \tan x = (\sec x)^2\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \cot x = -(\csc x)^2\)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \sec x = \sec x \tan x \)

  • \(\displaystyle \frac{d}{dx} \csc x = -\csc x \cot x \)

Activity 2.4.16.

Consider the functions

\begin{equation*} f(x)=3 \, \cos\left(x\right), \, \, g(x)=x^{2} + 3 \, e^{x} \end{equation*}

and the function \(h(x)\) for which a table of values is given.

\begin{equation*} \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} x & -1& 0& 2 \\\hline h(x) & -4& -1& 3 \\\hline h'(x) & 0& -1& 1 \\ \end{array} \end{equation*}

In answering the following questions, be sure to explicitly denote which derivative rules (product, quotient, sum and difference, etc.) you are using in your work.

  1. Find the derivative of \(f(x)\cdot g(x)\text{.}\)

  2. Find the derivative of \(\displaystyle \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\text{.}\)

  3. Find the value of the derivative of \(f(x) \cdot h(x) \) at \(x=-1\text{.}\)

  4. Find the value of the derivative of \(\displaystyle \frac{g(x)}{h(x)}\) at \(x=0\text{.}\)

  5. Consider the function

    \begin{equation*} r(x) = 3 \, \cos\left(x\right) \cdot x . \end{equation*}

    Find \(r'(x)\text{,}\) \(r''(x)\text{,}\) \(r'''(x)\text{,}\) and \(r^{(iv)}(x)\) so the first, second, third, and fourth derivative of \(r(x)\text{.}\) What pattern do you notice? What do you expect the twelfth derivative of \(r(x)\) to be?

Answer.

  1. \(\displaystyle (fg)'(x)=3 \, {\left(2 \, x + 3 \, e^{x}\right)} \cos\left(x\right) - 3 \, {\left(x^{2} + 3 \, e^{x}\right)} \sin\left(x\right)\)

  2. \(\displaystyle (f/g)'(x)=-\frac{3 \, {\left({\left(2 \, x + 3 \, e^{x}\right)} \cos\left(x\right) + {\left(x^{2} + 3 \, e^{x}\right)} \sin\left(x\right)\right)}}{{\left(x^{2} + 3 \, e^{x}\right)}^{2}}\)

  3. \(\displaystyle -12 \, \sin\left(1\right)\)

  4. \(\displaystyle 0\)

  5. We have

    • \(\displaystyle r(x)=3 \, \cos\left(x\right) \cdot x\)

    • \(\displaystyle r'(x)=-3 \, x \sin\left(x\right) + 3 \, \cos\left(x\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle r''(x)=-3 \, x \cos\left(x\right) - 6 \, \sin\left(x\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle r'''(x)=3 \, x \sin\left(x\right) - 9 \, \cos\left(x\right)\)

    • \(\displaystyle r^{(iv)}=3 \, x \cos\left(x\right) + 12 \, \sin\left(x\right)\)

    The functions involved repeat every four derivatives. So the twelth derivative should have the summand \(3 \, x \cos\left(x\right)\text{.}\) The second summand has a coefficient that increases by 3 every time, so the twelth derivative should have coefficient 36. Putting this together \(r^{(xii)}=3 \, x \cos\left(x\right) + 36 \, \sin\left(x\right)\)

\begin{equation*} f(140) = 15000, \, \, \, f'(140)= -100 \end{equation*}

(a)

(b)

(c)

\begin{equation*} \frac{dR}{dp}\Big|_{p=140}. \end{equation*}

(d)

(b)
